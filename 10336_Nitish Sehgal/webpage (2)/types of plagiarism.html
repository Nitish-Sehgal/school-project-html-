Types of plagiarism

type discription
direct plagiarism Direct plagiarism is when a passage of someone else's writing is copied verbatim, without attribution, and without using quote marks. The intentional theft of another person's work is unethical, dishonest in the classroom, and grounds for disciplinary punishment, including expulsion.
Self Plagiarism Self-plagiarism occurs when a student uploads their own prior work or incorporates pieces of prior works without the consent of all relevant professors. It would be improper.
Mosaic Plagiarism When a student uses terms from a source without putting them in quote marks or comes up with synonyms for the author's words while adhering to the original's overall organisation and meaning, they are committing mosaic plagiarism. Even if you footnote your source, this type of paraphrase, sometimes referred to as "patch writing," is academically dishonest and criminal
Accidental Plagiarism Accidental plagiarism happens when a person forgets to cite their sources, incorrectly paraphrases them, or unintentionally uses similar terms, word groupings, or sentence structures without giving proper credit. When conducting research, students must learn how to properly credit their sources and take thorough notes. Plagiarism committed by a student is still their duty even if they didn't mean to. Accidental plagiarism is treated with the same seriousness as other cases of plagiarism and is subject to the same range of penalties.
